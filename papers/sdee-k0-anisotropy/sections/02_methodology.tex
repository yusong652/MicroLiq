\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{DEM simulation of hollow cylinder tests}
\label{subsec:dem_hca}

DEM simulations were performed in \textsc{PFC3D} to reproduce undrained cyclic torsional shear in a hollow cylinder apparatus (HCA). Unlike periodic-cell element tests, the present HCA model uses rigid boundaries (inner and outer cylinders, top and bottom planes) and torsional blades to apply shear while preserving laboratory-relevant boundary conditions.

The specimen geometry follows a laboratory-scale HCA configuration with inner diameter \SI{6}{cm}, outer diameter \SI{10}{cm}, and specimen height \SI{10}{cm}. A rolling-resistance contact model is adopted to capture non-spherical grain effects, with a uniform particle size distribution in the range of \SIrange{1.5}{3.0}{mm}. Particles are generated by gravity deposition from the upper part of the apparatus, after which torsional blades are inserted to enable torque application.

\subsection{Specimen preparation and anisotropic consolidation protocol}
\label{subsec:preparation}

Specimens are prepared using an isotropic-consolidation to anisotropic-consolidation (IC--AC) protocol. First, the assembly is compressed to a target void ratio (approximately 0.736) under zero interparticle friction to obtain a dense and reproducible initial packing. The interparticle friction coefficient is then restored, and the specimen is consolidated from an isotropic state (\Kzero{} = 1.0, $\pmean=\SI{10}{kPa}$) to the target mean effective stress ($\pmean=\SI{100}{kPa}$) along a linear anisotropic stress path.

Three representative \Kzero{} states are analyzed in the primary set of simulations: \Kzero{} = 0.5 (compression), 1.0 (isotropic), and 2.0 (extension). During the AC stage, both $\pmean$ and deviator stress increase simultaneously. The resulting stress paths and void-ratio evolution are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:ac_path}. The final void ratio differs only slightly among the three states (on the order of $10^{-3}$), which allows the comparison to focus on fabric-related effects rather than void-ratio differences.

\subsection{Undrained cyclic torsional shear loading}
\label{subsec:cyclic_loading}

Undrained conditions are represented using the standard constant-volume approximation in DEM, without explicit fluid--particle coupling. To impose laboratory-like HCA boundary conditions under undrained loading, a combined servo mechanism is used to solve for the boundary kinematics at each timestep.

The servo simultaneously controls four kinematic variables (inner radius rate, outer radius rate, specimen height rate, and rotational rate) to satisfy: (i) equal inner and outer chamber pressures, (ii) axial loading condition (constant additional axial pressure or constant height), (iii) target shear stress, and (iv) constant volume. The constant-volume constraint is written as
\begin{equation}
2\pi H\left(R\frac{dR}{dt} + r\frac{dr}{dt}\right) + \pi\left(R^2-r^2\right)\frac{dH}{dt} = 0.
\label{eq:const_volume_constraint}
\end{equation}
The torsional servo coefficient is determined from the contact-stiffness-weighted rotational stiffness about the specimen axis.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \safeincludegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{servo_torsional_contact_geometry.png}
  \caption{Contact geometry used to evaluate the rotational stiffness contribution for torsional servo control.}
  \label{fig:servo_geometry}
\end{figure}

For cyclic loading, specimens are subjected to sinusoidal torsional shear under constant-height condition ($dH/dt=0$). The cyclic stress ratio (CSR) is defined using the applied maximum torsional shear stress normalized by the initial mean effective stress. In the primary analyses, liquefaction is identified using a stress-based criterion, $\ru = 0.95$, where $\ru$ is the excess pore pressure ratio. A strain-based criterion (single-amplitude shear strain $\gamma_{z\theta}=2.5\%$) is also evaluated to confirm consistency of the liquefaction resistance ordering.

The excess pore water pressure is inferred from the reduction in radial effective stress:
\begin{equation}
u = \sigma_{r0}^{\prime} - \sigma_r^{\prime},
\label{eq:epwp_definition}
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the excess pore water pressure, $\sigma_r^{\prime}$ is the current radial effective stress, and $\sigma_{r0}^{\prime}$ is its initial value. The ratio $\ru$ is then defined as $\ru = u/\sigma_{r0}^{\prime}$.

\subsection{Parameter calibration and validation}
\label{subsec:calibration_validation}

Micro-parameters were calibrated iteratively against dense Toyoura sand HCA data to reproduce (i) undrained monotonic stiffness, (ii) dilatancy response in effective-stress space, and (iii) cyclic liquefaction resistance. The calibration procedure adjusted contact modulus and rolling resistance while checking consistency between stress--strain response and effective-stress-path evolution.

The calibrated parameter set was validated against HCA experiments for dense Toyoura sand ($D_r \approx 80\%$, $\pmean_0=\SI{100}{kPa}$, \Kzero{} = 1.0) under both monotonic and cyclic loading. Monotonic torsional shear simulations were compared with data reported by Nakata et al., and cyclic validation was performed using CSR--$N_L$ data from Ishihara et al., with liquefaction defined by $\gamma_{z\theta}=2.5\%$.

\subsection{Microscopic descriptors}
\label{subsec:micro_descriptors}

Two microscopic descriptors are used to interpret the observed \Kzero{} dependence of liquefaction resistance: the mechanical coordination number \Zm{} and the signed fabric anisotropy indicator $\alpha$.

The mechanical coordination number is defined as
\begin{equation}
Z_m = \frac{2N_c - N_1}{N_p - N_1 - N_0},
\label{eq:zm_definition_method}
\end{equation}
where floaters are excluded to focus on the load-bearing contact network.

To distinguish compression-type and extension-type anisotropy, the signed scalar indicator $\alpha$ is adopted from the fabric tensor principal component in the vertical direction:
\begin{equation}
\alpha = \frac{5(3\varphi_v - 1)}{5\varphi_v + 1},
\label{eq:alpha_definition_method}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_v$ is the vertical principal component of the contact-normal fabric tensor. Positive $\alpha$ indicates axial concentration of contact normals, whereas negative $\alpha$ indicates circumferential concentration.

These descriptors are used together with contact-density and force-chain visualizations to connect particle-scale fabric evolution to the macroscopic liquefaction resistance trends reported in \cref{sec:results}.
