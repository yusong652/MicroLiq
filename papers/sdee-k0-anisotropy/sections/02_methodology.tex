\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{DEM simulation of hollow cylinder tests}
\label{subsec:dem_hca}

DEM simulations were performed in \textsc{PFC3D} \citep{Itasca2021} to reproduce undrained cyclic torsional shear in a hollow cylinder apparatus (HCA). Unlike periodic-cell element tests, the present HCA model uses rigid boundaries (inner and outer cylinders, top and bottom planes) and torsional blades to apply shear while preserving laboratory-relevant boundary conditions.

The specimen geometry follows a laboratory-scale HCA configuration with inner diameter \SI{6}{cm}, outer diameter \SI{10}{cm}, and specimen height \SI{10}{cm}. A rolling-resistance contact model \citep{IwashitaOda1998} is adopted to capture non-spherical grain effects, with a uniform particle size distribution in the range of \SIrange{1.5}{3.0}{mm}. The DEM parameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:dem_parameters}. Particles are generated by gravity deposition from the upper part of the apparatus, after which torsional blades are inserted to enable torque application.

\subsection{Specimen preparation and anisotropic consolidation protocol}
\label{subsec:preparation}

Specimens are prepared using an isotropic-consolidation to anisotropic-consolidation (IC--AC) protocol. First, the assembly is compressed to a target void ratio under zero interparticle friction to obtain a reproducible initial packing: approximately 0.736 for the dense state ($D_r \approx 90\%$) and 0.742 for the medium-dense state ($D_r \approx 60\%$). The interparticle friction coefficient is then restored, and the specimen is consolidated from an isotropic state (\Kzero{} = 1.0, $\pmean=\SI{10}{kPa}$) to the target mean effective stress ($\pmean=\SI{100}{kPa}$) along a linear anisotropic stress path.

The simulation program consists of two sets. In the primary set, three representative \Kzero{} states---0.5 (compression), 1.0 (isotropic), and 2.0 (extension)---are analyzed at a dense state ($D_r \approx 90\%$) across multiple CSR levels to establish the macroscopic liquefaction resistance ordering and to examine the associated energy, stiffness, and fabric evolution. In the expanded set, two relative densities ($D_r \approx 90\%$ and $60\%$) are combined with five \Kzero{} values (0.5, 0.67, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0) at a single CSR = 0.300, yielding ten cases designed to disentangle the effects of packing compactness and directional fabric anisotropy on liquefaction resistance.

During the AC stage, both $\pmean$ and deviator stress increase simultaneously. The resulting stress paths and void-ratio evolution for the three primary cases are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:ac_path}. The final void ratio differs only slightly among the three states (on the order of $10^{-3}$), which allows the comparison to focus on fabric-related effects rather than void-ratio differences.

\subsection{Undrained cyclic torsional shear loading}
\label{subsec:cyclic_loading}

Undrained conditions are represented using the standard constant-volume approximation in DEM, without explicit fluid--particle coupling \citep{YimsiriSoga2010}. The key challenge in simulating undrained HCA tests lies in satisfying multiple boundary conditions simultaneously. In laboratory tests, the inner and outer chamber pressures are maintained equal, the axial condition is controlled, a target shear stress is applied, and the specimen volume remains constant. This study addresses these challenges through a combined servo mechanism \citep{Ma2024} that controls four kinematic variables---inner radius rate ($dr/dt$), outer radius rate ($dR/dt$), height rate ($dH/dt$), and rotation angle rate ($d\theta/dt$)---to satisfy four condition equations simultaneously at each timestep.

\paragraph{Condition 1: Equal inner and outer chamber pressures.}
To address the radial inhomogeneity caused by the axial symmetry of HCA, the difference in effective stresses between the outer and inner cylinders is regulated by controlling the inner radius rate:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{\sigma_{dif,r}^{\prime} - (p_o - p_i)}{\Delta t}\,S_{cr},
\label{eq:cond1_radial}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{dif,r}^{\prime}$ is the effective stress difference between the outer and inner cylinders, and $S_{cr}$ is the radial servo coefficient.

\paragraph{Condition 2: Constant height.}
During undrained cyclic shear, the height is maintained constant ($dH/dt = 0$). Alternatively, if additional axial pressure control is desired, the height rate can be governed by a servo equation analogous to \cref{eq:cond1_radial}.

\paragraph{Condition 3: Target shear stress.}
The rotation angle rate is controlled to achieve the target shear stress:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\theta}{dt} = \frac{T_{dif}}{\Delta t}\,S_{cs},
\label{eq:cond3_torsion}
\end{equation}
where $T_{dif}$ is the torque difference between the target and current values, and $S_{cs}$ is the torsional servo coefficient. The coefficient $S_{cs}$ is determined from the contact-stiffness-weighted rotational stiffness $I_{rot}$ about the specimen axis (Fig.~\ref{fig:servo_geometry}):
\begin{equation}
I_{rot} = \sum r_d^2\,k_n\cos^2\!\theta, \qquad S_{cs} = \frac{1}{I_{rot}},
\label{eq:irot_scs}
\end{equation}
where $r_d$ is the distance from the rotation axis to the contact point, $k_n$ is the normal contact stiffness, and $\theta$ is the angle between the contact normal and the horizontal plane.

\paragraph{Condition 4: Undrained condition (constant volume).}
The outer radius rate $dR/dt$ is determined from the constant-volume constraint:
\begin{equation}
2\pi H\!\left(R\frac{dR}{dt} + r\frac{dr}{dt}\right) + \pi\!\left(R^2-r^2\right)\frac{dH}{dt} = 0.
\label{eq:const_volume_constraint}
\end{equation}
With $dr/dt$ known from \cref{eq:cond1_radial} and $dH/dt$ from Condition~2, \cref{eq:const_volume_constraint} is solved for $dR/dt$. Together, the four conditions form a closed system that uniquely determines the four kinematic variables at each timestep, thereby simultaneously realizing the stress boundary conditions and undrained constraint of laboratory HCA tests.

\paragraph{Cyclic loading protocol.}
Specimens are subjected to sinusoidal torsional shear under the constant-height condition ($dH/dt=0$). The cyclic stress ratio (CSR) is defined as the maximum applied torsional shear stress normalized by the initial mean effective stress. In the primary analyses, liquefaction is identified using a stress-based criterion, $\ru = 0.95$, where $\ru$ is the excess pore pressure ratio. A strain-based criterion (single-amplitude shear strain $\gamma_{z\theta}=2.5\%$) is also evaluated to confirm consistency of the liquefaction resistance ordering.

The excess pore water pressure is inferred from the reduction in radial effective stress:
\begin{equation}
u = \sigma_{r0}^{\prime} - \sigma_r^{\prime},
\label{eq:epwp_definition}
\end{equation}
where $u$ is the excess pore water pressure, $\sigma_r^{\prime}$ is the current radial effective stress, and $\sigma_{r0}^{\prime}$ is its initial value. The ratio $\ru$ is then defined as $\ru = u/\sigma_{r0}^{\prime}$.

\subsection{Parameter calibration and validation}
\label{subsec:calibration_validation}

Micro-parameters were calibrated iteratively against dense Toyoura sand HCA data to reproduce (i) undrained monotonic stiffness, (ii) dilatancy response in effective-stress space, and (iii) cyclic liquefaction resistance. The calibration procedure adjusted contact modulus and rolling resistance while checking consistency between stress--strain response and effective-stress-path evolution.

The calibrated parameter set was validated against HCA experiments for dense Toyoura sand ($D_r \approx 90\%$, $\pmean_0=\SI{100}{kPa}$, \Kzero{} = 1.0) under both monotonic and cyclic loading. Monotonic torsional shear simulations were compared with data reported by \citet{Nakata1998}, and cyclic validation was performed using CSR--$N_L$ data from \citet{Ishihara1985}, with liquefaction defined by $\gamma_{z\theta}=2.5\%$.

\subsection{Microscopic descriptors}
\label{subsec:micro_descriptors}

Three microscopic descriptors are used to interpret the observed \Kzero{} dependence of liquefaction resistance: the mechanical coordination number \Zm{}, the signed fabric anisotropy indicator $\alpha$, and the contact density $\rho_c$.

The mechanical coordination number \citep{Thornton2000,Oda1977} is defined as
\begin{equation}
Z_m = \frac{2N_c - N_1}{N_p - N_1 - N_0},
\label{eq:zm_definition_method}
\end{equation}
where $N_c$ is the total number of interparticle contacts, $N_p$ is the total number of particles, and $N_1$ and $N_0$ are the numbers of particles with one and zero contacts, respectively. Floaters are excluded to focus on the load-bearing contact network.

The directionality of the contact network is characterized through the fabric tensor \citep{Oda1982fabric,Oda1982stat}:
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{ij} = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum n_i\,n_j,
\label{eq:fabric_tensor}
\end{equation}
where $n_i$ and $n_j$ are the components of the contact normal vector. To distinguish compression-type and extension-type anisotropy, the signed scalar indicator $\alpha$ \citep{YimsiriSoga2010,Otsubo2022} is defined from the vertical principal component $\varphi_v$ of $\Phi_{ij}$:
\begin{equation}
\alpha = \frac{5(3\varphi_v - 1)}{5\varphi_v + 1}.
\label{eq:alpha_definition_method}
\end{equation}
Positive $\alpha$ indicates axial concentration of contact normals (compression-state anisotropy, \Kzero{} $< 1.0$), whereas negative $\alpha$ indicates circumferential concentration (extension-state anisotropy, \Kzero{} $> 1.0$).

The contact density \citep{Han2023} describes the average number of contacts per unit surface area on a particle with normalized radius:
\begin{equation}
\rho_c(\theta_z,\,\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}) = \frac{2N_{\theta_z,\,\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}}}{N_p\displaystyle\int_{\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}}^{\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}+\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}}\!\sin\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}\,d\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}\int_{\theta_z}^{\theta_z+\Delta\theta_z}\!d\theta_z},
\label{eq:contact_density}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_z$ and $\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}$ are the polar and azimuthal angles in the spherical coordinate system, and $N_{\theta_z,\,\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}}$ is the number of contacts whose normals fall within the angular bin $(\theta_z,\,\theta_z+\Delta\theta_z)\times(\phi_{\mathrm{cir}},\,\phi_{\mathrm{cir}}+\Delta\phi_{\mathrm{cir}})$.

These descriptors are used together with force-chain visualizations to connect particle-scale fabric evolution to the macroscopic liquefaction resistance trends reported in \cref{sec:results}.
