\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Liquefaction, namely the loss of soil strength due to excess pore-pressure buildup during earthquake loading, can cause severe damage to infrastructure and buildings. A large body of laboratory work has investigated liquefaction resistance using cyclic undrained triaxial tests, where saturated specimens are loaded until liquefaction and the effects of cyclic stress ratio (CSR), density, and confining pressure are evaluated. However, vertically propagating shear waves in the ground induce stress paths with continuous rotation of principal stress axes, which cannot be fully represented in conventional triaxial loading.

Hollow cylinder apparatus (HCA) and hollow torsional shear tests address this limitation by allowing torsional loading with principal stress axis rotation. Early studies using HCA-type devices \citep[e.g.,][]{IshiharaYasuda1975,Tatsuoka1986,YamashitaToki1993} showed that liquefaction behavior can differ from triaxial-test results and depends on the loading mode and stress path. These observations motivate the use of HCA-type boundary conditions when investigating liquefaction mechanisms under more realistic cyclic loading paths.

The influence of the initial lateral-to-vertical effective stress ratio, \Kzero{}, on liquefaction resistance remains controversial. Some experimental studies reported weak or negligible dependence of liquefaction strength on the initial anisotropic stress state \citep[e.g.,][]{IshiharaTakatsu1979,YamashitaToki1993}, whereas others found clear differences between isotropically consolidated (IC) and anisotropically consolidated (AC) specimens, with the trend depending on density \citep[e.g.,][]{GeorgiannouKonstadinou2014,Vargas2020}. Despite decades of investigation, the particle-scale mechanisms through which stress anisotropy alters liquefaction resistance remain insufficiently clarified.

The discrete element method (DEM) is well suited for this problem because it provides direct access to particle-scale quantities such as contact topology, force chains, and fabric anisotropy, while avoiding some specimen-to-specimen variability associated with laboratory preparation. Numerous DEM studies have examined liquefaction-related behavior in triaxial or simple-shear settings and have identified important microscopic factors governing liquefaction resistance. However, DEM simulations that explicitly reproduce HCA-type boundary conditions under undrained cyclic torsional shear remain relatively limited, particularly for systematic investigation of \Kzero{} effects.

DEM replication of HCA tests has precedents under drained loading \citep[e.g.,][]{Li2014,Liu2021}, but extension to undrained cyclic loading in HCA geometry remains limited. Building on recent developments in DEM-based HCA simulation \citep{Ma2024}, the present study employs a combined servo mechanism that simultaneously satisfies the HCA stress boundary conditions and the constant-volume constraint as a proxy for the undrained condition.

The present work investigates the effect of initial stress anisotropy on liquefaction resistance in DEM-simulated undrained cyclic torsional shear of hollow cylindrical specimens. Specimens are prepared using an isotropic-consolidation to anisotropic-consolidation (IC--AC) stress path, in which initial isotropic consolidation is followed by linear anisotropic consolidation from $\pmean=\SI{10}{kPa}$ to $\pmean=\SI{100}{kPa}$ to reach the target \Kzero{} state. The investigation is organized in two stages. First, the macroscopic \Kzero{}-dependent liquefaction resistance trend is established using three representative cases (\Kzero{} = 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0, corresponding to compression, isotropic, and extension states) at a dense state ($D_r \approx 90\%$) across multiple CSR levels, accompanied by macroscopic energy and stiffness analyses and microscopic fabric evolution. Second, to further disentangle the roles of packing compactness and directional fabric anisotropy, the simulation matrix is expanded to two relative densities ($D_r \approx 90\%$ and $60\%$) and five \Kzero{} values (0.5, 0.67, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0), yielding ten cases examined at a single CSR. This two-stage approach first confirms the macroscopic \Kzero{} ordering and then clarifies, through the expanded parametric set, how the signed fabric anisotropy indicator $\alpha_0$ governs the observed trends independently of coordination number.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} presents the DEM-HCA model, specimen preparation and loading protocol, calibration/validation strategy, and microscopic descriptors. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the macroscopic and microscopic responses and discusses the mechanisms governing \Kzero{}-dependent liquefaction resistance. Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the main findings and implications.
