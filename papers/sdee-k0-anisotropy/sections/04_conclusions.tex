\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This study used DEM simulations of undrained cyclic torsional shear in hollow cylindrical specimens to investigate how initial stress anisotropy (\Kzero{}) affects liquefaction resistance under HCA-type boundary conditions. The main findings are summarized as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Liquefaction resistance exhibits a clear directional dependency on initial stress anisotropy. Compression-state anisotropy (\Kzero{} = 0.5) produces the highest resistance, the isotropic state (\Kzero{} = 1.0) shows intermediate resistance, and extension-state anisotropy (\Kzero{} = 2.0) yields the lowest resistance across the CSR range considered.

\item The difference in liquefaction resistance cannot be explained solely by the total cumulative shear work at liquefaction. While normalized energy accumulation patterns are similar among the three \Kzero{} states, the per-cycle energy input rate differs because of distinct stiffness degradation trajectories. The compression-state specimen degrades stiffness more slowly, whereas the extension-state specimen degrades fastest and liquefies in fewer cycles.

\item The mechanical coordination number \Zm{} primarily captures the effect of packing compactness and helps explain the difference between density levels, but does not fully account for the \Kzero{} effect within a given density state. The signed fabric anisotropy indicator $\alpha$ and the corresponding contact-density morphology distinguish compression-type and extension-type anisotropy and correlate more directly with the variation in liquefaction resistance among \Kzero{} states.

\item Contact-density distributions and force-chain visualizations show that initially distinct fabric morphologies progressively degrade and converge toward similar configurations near and after liquefaction, indicating that cyclic loading drives the granular assembly toward a common post-liquefaction fabric state regardless of the initial stress anisotropy.

\item The combination of macroscopic indicators (CSR--$N_L$, cumulative shear work, shear-wave velocity) and microscopic descriptors (\Zm{}, $\alpha$, contact density) provides a practical framework for interpreting \Kzero{}-dependent liquefaction resistance in DEM simulations of hollow cylinder tests.
\end{enumerate}
