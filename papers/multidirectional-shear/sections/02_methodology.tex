\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{DEM model and specimen preparation}
\label{subsec:dem_model}

DEM simulations were performed using \textsc{PFC3D} \citep{Itasca2021}. Spherical particles with diameters ranging from \SIrange{1.0}{3.0}{mm}, approximating the grain size distribution of Toyoura sand (Fig.~\ref{fig:specimen_configuration}a), were generated within a cuboidal domain enclosed by periodic boundaries in the two horizontal directions and rigid ribbed shear walls in the vertical direction. A rolling-resistance contact model \citep{IwashitaOda1998,WensrichKatterfeld2012} was adopted to capture the mechanical effects of non-spherical grain shape. The DEM parameters are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:dem_parameters}.

Specimen preparation followed a two-stage procedure. First, particles were deposited under gravity, and the specimen was then compacted to an isotropic stress state with $p'_0 = \SI{100}{kPa}$ using a servo-controlled wall adjustment. Six vertically oriented shear blades were inserted through the specimen to transmit torsional shear force (Fig.~\ref{fig:specimen_configuration}b). Undrained (constant-volume) conditions were enforced throughout cyclic loading via a servo mechanism, and excess pore water pressure was computed from the difference between the current and initial mean effective stresses.

\subsection{Loading path design}
\label{subsec:loading_paths}

The central methodological contribution of this study is a systematic loading path design that progressively eliminates confounding factors, allowing shear stress directionality to be isolated as the sole variable. Three loading types are compared: single-8, equivalent-magnitude unidirectional, and double-8.

\paragraph{Single-8 shear stress path.}
The multidirectional single-8 (figure-8) path consists of a sinusoidal component in the $x$-direction and a second sinusoidal component in the $y$-direction at half the amplitude and twice the frequency:
\begin{align}
  \tau_{8,x} &= \mathrm{CSR} \cdot \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi t}{T}\right) p'_0
  \label{eq:tau_8x} \\
  \tau_{8,y} &= \frac{\mathrm{CSR}}{2} \cdot \sin\!\left(\frac{4\pi t}{T}\right) p'_0
  \label{eq:tau_8y}
\end{align}
where $T$ is the loading period, $\mathrm{CSR}$ is the cyclic stress ratio, and $p'_0$ is the initial mean effective stress. The resulting stress path traces a figure-8 pattern in the $\tau_x$--$\tau_y$ plane (Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_paths}c).

\paragraph{Equivalent-magnitude unidirectional path.}
To ensure that the instantaneous shear stress magnitude is identical between unidirectional and multidirectional loading at every moment, the unidirectional stress is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \tau_{\mathrm{uni},x}
    = \mathrm{sign}(\tau_{8,x})\,
      \sqrt{\tau_{8,x}^2 + \tau_{8,y}^2}
  \label{eq:tau_uni}
\end{equation}
This ensures that the only difference between the unidirectional and single-8 paths is the direction of the shear stress (Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_paths}a,\,b).

\paragraph{Double-8 shear stress path.}
Although the magnitudes of the unidirectional and single-8 stresses are equal, their rates of change differ fundamentally because the multidirectional path includes continuous directional variation. To eliminate this confounding factor, the double-8 path swaps the $x$- and $y$-components between odd and even cycles:
\begin{align}
  \tau_{d8,x} &= \begin{cases}
    \tau_{8,x}, & 2nT < t \leq (2n+1)T \\
    \tau_{8,y}, & (2n+1)T < t \leq 2(n+1)T
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:tau_d8x} \\[4pt]
  \tau_{d8,y} &= \begin{cases}
    \tau_{8,y}, & 2nT < t \leq (2n+1)T \\
    \tau_{8,x}, & (2n+1)T < t \leq 2(n+1)T
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:tau_d8y}
\end{align}
where $n \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$. This construction ensures that (1)~the stress magnitude of the double-8 path equals that of the single-8 path at every instant, (2)~the rates of change are equal in magnitude (opposite in sign during even cycles), and (3)~the phase transition between odd and even cycles produces a smooth, continuous stress history (Fig.~\ref{fig:stress_paths}d).

\paragraph{Summary of controlled variables.}
Table~\ref{tab:controlled_variables} summarizes the variables controlled in each pairwise comparison. By comparing unidirectional versus single-8, the combined effect of stress directionality and rate variation is assessed. By comparing single-8 versus double-8---where both magnitude and rate are matched---the isolated effect of loading history (periodic axis alternation) is revealed.

\subsection{Parameter calibration and validation}
\label{subsec:calibration_validation}

The DEM micro-parameters were calibrated using monotonic undrained shear test data on dense Toyoura sand ($D_r \approx 80\%$, $p'_0 = \SI{100}{kPa}$) reported by \citet{Nakata1998}. Despite inherent differences between the DEM model (periodic boundaries, idealized particle shapes) and the laboratory hollow cylinder specimens (non-uniform stress distribution across specimen thickness), the comparison remains meaningful as both configurations target simple shear deformation under undrained conditions.

  \hfill

  \caption{Validation of DEM simulation against monotonic undrained shear tests
    \citep{Nakata1998}: dense Toyoura sand with $D_r \approx 80\%$,
    $p'_0 = \SI{100}{kPa}$.
    (a)~Effective stress path. (b)~Stress--strain relationship.}
  \label{fig:validation}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}, the simulation successfully reproduces the characteristic undrained response of dense sand: (1)~dilative behavior in effective stress space, with the stress path initially moving leftward as $p'$ decreases, then reversing as dilation develops; and (2)~strain-hardening, with deviatoric stress $q$ increasing monotonically with strain. Close agreement is achieved in the small-strain regime ($\varepsilon_q < 0.5\%$), where both the initial stiffness and the contractive--dilative transition are well captured. At larger strains, the simulation follows the experimental trend while exhibiting slightly higher deviatoric stress, which is attributed to the idealized periodic boundary conditions.


\subsection{Microscopic descriptors}
\label{subsec:micro_descriptors}

Three microscopic quantities are used to characterize fabric evolution during cyclic loading.

\paragraph{Mechanical coordination number.}
The mechanical coordination number $\Zm$ measures the average number of load-bearing contacts per particle, excluding floaters (particles with fewer than two contacts):
\begin{equation}
  \Zm = \frac{2N_c - N_1}{N_p - N_1 - N_0}
  \label{eq:zm}
\end{equation}
where $N_c$ is the total number of contacts, $N_p$ is the total number of particles, and $N_1$ and $N_0$ are the numbers of particles with one and zero contacts, respectively \citep{Thornton2000}.

\paragraph{Fabric anisotropy invariant.}
The second-order fabric tensor $\Phi_{ij}$ is computed as the average outer product of contact normal vectors over all contacts:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{ij} = \frac{1}{N_c} \sum_{k=1}^{N_c} n_i^{(k)} n_j^{(k)}
  \label{eq:fabric_tensor}
\end{equation}
The deviatoric (anisotropic) part is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  F_{ij} = \frac{15}{2}\!\left(\Phi_{ij} - \frac{\delta_{ij}}{3}\right)
  \label{eq:deviatoric_fabric}
\end{equation}
and its scalar invariant quantifies the degree of contact anisotropy:
\begin{equation}
  \Fc = \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}\, F_{ij}\, F_{ij}}
  \label{eq:fc}
\end{equation}
Higher values of $\Fc$ indicate stronger directional preference in the contact network \citep{Oda1982fabric}.

\paragraph{Contact density distribution.}
The three-dimensional distribution of contact orientations is visualized using spherical histograms, providing a direct representation of fabric directionality beyond the scalar measures above.
